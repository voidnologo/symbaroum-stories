The Chronicle

North of Ambria — north of the refugee camps and the rebuilt cities, north of the Queen's new roads and the Church of Prios with its ordered hymns and its careful silences — the forest of Davokar begins. It does not ease you in. One moment the world is broken but comprehensible: politics, poverty, survival. The next, the canopy closes and the light changes quality, and something behind the leaves has noticed you. Davokar is not wilderness. It is memory — ancient, corrupted, and very much alive.

Corruption is the toll the forest levies on those who touch its power. A theurg channels divine fire and her skin darkens where the blessings burn through her. A scholar reads a forbidden text and feels something shift behind his eyes, a door opening that will not close again. The more you reach for what Davokar offers — and it offers much — the more it reaches back. There are those who have taken too much, and they no longer pass for human. They are called abominations. Once, they were people with names.

This is the story of four who walked into the dark and have not yet stopped walking. Agathara Silvershade, Confessor of Prios, burns with holy light she cannot always control. Every miracle she works carves another mark into her — but she works them anyway, because the alternative is to let the dark have what it wants. Karano, an Ironsworn hunter of singular calm, carries nothing that Davokar can corrupt. He has walked through storms that crippled his companions and emerged without a mark, not because he is protected, but because he has nothing in him that the forest knows how to take. Reginald is a scholar who opened a door in his mind in a library he was not meant to enter, and now sees the world laid over itself in amber and silver light — Witch Sight, they call it. He is still deciding if it was worth it. And then there is Aro Sunspear, a patron saint who should be dead and is not, flickering at the edges of rooms like a candle someone forgot to extinguish, still fighting, still illuminating the path ahead.

These pages hold moments from their years together — each illustration a still point in a story that has not ended. Click any scene to read what happened there.
